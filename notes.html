Chapter 6 Moral Philosophy - refers to the specific principles or values people use to decide what is right and wrong. Economic value orientation - if an act produces more value for its effort, then it should be accepted as ethical. Idealism - is a moral philosophy that places special value on ideas and ideals as products of the mind Realism - is the view that an external world exists independent of our perceptions. Monists - believe only one thing is intrinsically good. Pluralists - believe two or more things are intriniscally good. Hedonism - the ideal that pleasure is the ultimate good. Teleology - stipulates acts are morally right or acceptable if they produce some desired result, such as realization of self-interest or utility Egoism - Defines right or acceptable actions as those that maximise a particular person's self-interest as defined by the individual. Utiliarianism - Defines right or acceptable actions as those that maximize total utility, or the greatest good for the greatest number of people. Deotology - Focuses on the preservation of individuals rights and on the intentions associated with particular behavior rather than on its consequences. Relavist - Evaluates ethicalness subjectively on the basis of individual and group experiences. Virture ethics - Assumes what is moral in a given situation is not only what conventional morality requires but also what the mature person with a "good" moral character deems appropriate. Justice - Evaluates ethicalness on the basis of fairness: distributive, procedural, and interactional. instrumentalists reject the ideas that: 1 ends can be seperated from the means that produce them and 2 ends, purposes, or outcomes are intrinsically good in and of themselves. Chapter 7 Apathetic Culture - Shows minimal concern for either people or performance. Caring culture - exhibits high concern for people but minimal concern for performance issues. Exacting Culture - shows little concern for people but a high concern for performance with a focus on the interest of an organization. Integrative Culture - Combines a high concern for people and performance Cultural Audit - is an assessment of an organization's values. Compliance Culture - is organized around risk, using a legalistic approach to ethics. Value-Based Ethics Culture - relies upon an explicit mision statement that defines the core values of the firm and how customers and employees should be treated.
